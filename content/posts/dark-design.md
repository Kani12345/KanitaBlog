# Dark design pattern 
Dark design, also known as dark patterns, refers to manipulative design techniques used in websites and apps to deceive or coerce users into taking actions they may not want to take. These designs exploit user behavior and psychology, often prioritizing company goals like maximizing profit over user interests. Common dark patterns include making subscription cancellations difficult, tricking users into signing up for services, or using guilt-laden language to influence decisions. While these tactics may boost short-term engagement, they erode user trust and can lead to frustration or dissatisfaction. Ethical design, in contrast, focuses on transparency and respecting user autonomy. 
In this article, we will focus on two dark design patterns: the roach motel and the confirm shaming We will also see how then can be redesigned to be more ethical. 

##  Roach Motel
The "Roach Motel" dark pattern refers to a situation where users can easily enter into a certain situation (e.g., signing up for a service), but find it difficult to get out (e.g., canceling the service). Companies make processes like subscription cancellation intentionally complex, keeping users trapped.

### Roach Motel example: Amazon prime subscription 
A typical "Roach Motel" design can be seen with Amazon Prime. It's easy to sign up for the service with just a few clicks, often bundled with other purchases (like free shipping offers). However, when you try to cancel, the process is long and convoluted. Amazon requires users to navigate through several screens with multiple confirmation steps to finally cancel, making it frustrating and time-consuming.

#### How to redesign it 
- Simplified Cancellation Process: To redesign this pattern, Amazon could place the "Cancel Subscription" button prominently on the account page and reduce the steps needed to unsubscribe to one or two screens. The first screen could ask for feedback on why the user is canceling, and the second screen could confirm the cancellation with a clear message.
- Confirmation and Follow-up: After cancelation, instead of multiple prompts trying to retain the user, they could be sent a friendly email offering information about their subscription status and easy reactivation options. This redesign focuses on transparency and respects the user’s choice. 

## Confirm shaming
"Confirmshaming" involves guilting or shaming users into making a choice they might not want. This typically occurs in the form of pop-ups or messages that offer options framed in such a way that declining seems socially or emotionally unacceptable.

### Confirmshaming example: unsubscribing from a newsletter
Imagine you’re on a retail site, and a pop-up appears asking you to subscribe to their newsletter. If you choose not to sign up, the decline button says something like “No thanks, I’d rather pay full price”, trying to guilt you into subscribing by implying you’re making a poor financial decision.

#### How to redesign it 
- Neutral, Respectful Language: Instead of the guilt-inducing message, the decline button could say something neutral like “No thanks, I’m not interested at the moment.” This change removes the pressure or shame and lets the user make an independent decision.
- Provide Real Value: Instead of using psychological pressure, the pop-up could explain the value of subscribing in a clear way, such as “Sign up to get exclusive discounts and new arrivals – join our community today!” This approach offers users a real reason to engage without shaming them for declining.


By redesigning these dark patterns, the goal is to create a user-friendly and ethical experience. Amazon Prime’s cancellation process could be streamlined and clear, while newsletter pop-ups could use respectful, value-driven language, fostering trust and positive engagement rather than manipulating users.
