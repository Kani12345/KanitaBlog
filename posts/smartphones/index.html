The shape of smartphones

November 1, 2024 3-minute read
Kanita

The shape of smartphones has continusly evolved since their introduction to the market: the blackberry phone is quite different from the last Iphone. This changes were mostly driven by changing user needs and technology improvements. Early smartphones were more compact, often featuring physical keyboards or smaller screens, like the BlackBerry and early iPhone models. As technology advanced, screens became larger, bezels shrank, and touch-based interfaces became the absolute norm. This shift in shape was influenced by the desire for a more immersive display experience, prompting manufacturers to design taller and narrower phones that are easier to hold but offer more screen space.

Why the Shape Changed Link to heading

  • Screen Size and User Experience: The desire for larger screens to enhance video, browsing, and gaming experiences led to designs like the 16:9, then 18:9, and even 21:9 aspect ratios, maximizing screen real estate while keeping phones manageable in hand.

  • Thinner Bezels and Rounded Edges: As technology allowed, bezels shrank, providing a more seamless screen-to-body experience. Rounded edges made phones more comfortable to hold and minimized accidental drops, which contributed to the trend of taller and slimmer designs.

  • Foldable and Flexible Displays: Recently, flexible OLED technology has allowed for foldable phones, like the Samsung Galaxy Fold and Motorola Razr. Foldables respond to the demand for larger screens in a more portable form, offering users both a compact device and a larger tablet-like experience in one. But they have yet to become the norm, cause it adds the inconvenience of “unfolding” the phone.

  • More and more performant camera: the newer smartphones have several objectives to adapt themselves to different type of photoraphy, where the first smartphones had only one and produced images of bad quality.

Next Steps in Smartphone Evolution Link to heading

  • Rollable Screens: A possible next step is the adoption of rollable displays, where the screen expands or retracts as needed. This would give users the flexibility of a larger screen without the bulk of a foldable. Brands like LG and TCL have already showcased rollable prototypes, where the screen extends from the side, expanding the viewing area while keeping the form factor compact.

  • Further Integration with wearables: Another direction could involve more integrated wearable screens. As wearables become more powerful, some functions traditionally done on a phone (like basic calls, notifications, and even video) may migrate to smaller, wrist-based or eyewear devices. This can already be done with devices such as connected watches, but there users are few. Impproved wearble could lead to thinner, smaller smartphones as a supplement to wearable tech rather than the central device.

  • Modular or Transformative Designs: In the longer term, we may see modular devices that users can customize based on specific needs. Concepts like magnetic attachment of screens or cameras could allow users to create phones that are optimized for photography, gaming, or work depending on their preferences. The same way we have computers specialised for gaming, we could have phones specialised in photography or gaming.

Why These Changes Are Likely Link to heading

These transformations respond to the growing user demand for versatility, portability, and functionality in one device. Rollable and modular designs reflect a shift toward customizable, on-the-go solutions without compromising screen size. As more users rely on phones as their primary devices for work and entertainment, manufacturers are likely to prioritize adaptability, expanding screen sizes only when necessary and integrating wearables for added functionality. The flexible form factors cater to users’ desires for larger screens while retaining the portability and comfort essential in a smartphone.
